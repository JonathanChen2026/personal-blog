**Modern college admissions content has devolved** into a chum bucket of AI-slop-level advice designed to prey on the fear of ambitious high schoolers... and it’s working.

I use social media - Instagram, Youtube (if that counts as social media) and occasionally Tiktok. While going through the college admissions cycle these past few months, naturally, a hefty portion of my for you page consisted of college admissions content, and I followed many of these creators.

While I discovered some useful nuggets of advice, albeit very infrequently, most of the content I saw was pretty much bullshit. "How could this useless reel get so viral?" I thought.

For admissions content to compete in such a saturated market, creators know that obvious advice no longer garners clicks or signs clients. Everyone knows you need a high gpa to get into the Ivy League, for example.
​

This has birthed a sloppy spectrum of advice where creators manufacture their own little twist and contradictory opinions.

One reel tells you that a 3.9+ is a prereq, while the next tells you can “substitute” a low gpa with a high SAT or even a “spike” in your activities section.

And so by constantly moving the goalposts, these creators ensure that the student remains in a perpetual state of confusion, allowing them to market their own paid consulting service or master course as the secret sauce.

The most notorious creator I've seen is Elise Pham. Let's use her as a case study. 

Elise is a first-gen Vietnamese American student at Harvard College (and Forbes 30 under 30 - that should tell you enough). She runs a college admissions consulting business called the Ultimate Ivy League Guide, which brought in $500,000 in revenue in 2024 and over $2 million in 2025. I’ve come across many of her TikTok and Instagram reels, with accounts at a combined 1.83 million followers.

If you just take a second to watch some of her content, you will understand her business model, and by extension, the modern college admissions industry. 
​

Most of her content features her speaking directly to the camera, right to the viewer.

Her videos function as a high-conversion funnel - they resonate with ambitious high schoolers’ insecurities, maybe low test scores or a stuck personal statement, before agitating them with a new "insider" strategy as the solution, a strategy that can only be accessed through one of her thousand dollar consulting packages. 

Back in the fall of last year, when I was applying to colleges, I remember watching one of her videos explaining that the common app has built in AI writing detection software, and that getting caught means the withdrawal of every single one of your applications. I actually believed it for a second.

Then I found it absolutely comical when THE common app company replied to her video and had to publicly correct the circulating misinformation. And her response video? No acknowledgement of her mistake, and continuing to market her product.

I understand that college admissions is a business and that creators have every right to monetize.  However, there is a difference between monetizing genuine expertise and monetizing panic-inducing slop. 

The current state of this industry is predatory and unhealthy, and as long as ambitious high schoolers are afraid of the future, there will always be someone waiting to sell them a scoop of the chum bucket.
